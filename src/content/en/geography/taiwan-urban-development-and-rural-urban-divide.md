# Taiwan's Urban Development and the Rural-Urban Divide

In just 70 years, Taiwan has rapidly transformed from an agricultural society to an urban society. The urban population ratio surged from 24% in 1950 to 79% in 2020—an unprecedented speed of urbanization that created prosperity while also bringing severe rural-urban disparities.

## The Journey of Post-War Urbanization

### 1950-1970s: The Dawn of Industrialization

Post-war Taiwan remained an agricultural society with 80% of the population engaged in farming, and cities were relatively small. Traditional cities like Taipei, Taichung, Tainan, and Kaohsiung served mainly administrative or port functions with limited populations.

The 1960s marked the beginning of industrialization policies, establishing export processing zones in Taoyuan, Hsinchu, and Taichung. These industrial zones attracted vast numbers of rural youth, becoming the first wave of Taiwan's urbanization. Many people migrated north from central and southern rural areas for work, creating inspiring stories of "workers becoming entrepreneurs."

Urban development during this period was relatively dispersed, without obvious urban concentration. While Taipei was the political center, economic activities were distributed across Taiwan. Kaohsiung, with its heavy industry development, once had the momentum to challenge Taipei's dominance.

### 1980-2000s: Service Industry Rise and Taipei's Dominance

The 1980s saw Taiwan's economic transformation from manufacturing to services. Financial, trade, and information services concentrated mostly in Taipei, establishing the "Taipei dominance" pattern.

The most obvious phenomenon during this period was the expansion of the "Taipei Metropolitan Area." As Taipei City gradually saturated, population and industries spread to surrounding New Taipei, Taoyuan, and Keelung. The construction of Taipei's MRT system further strengthened metropolitan integration, forming the Greater Taipei living circle.

Meanwhile, Taichung and Kaohsiung began developing as regional centers. Taichung became the central Taiwan hub due to its geographical location, while Kaohsiung maintained its southern Taiwan leadership through its port and heavy industries. However, both remained much smaller than the Taipei metropolitan area.

### 2000s to Present: Formation of the Six Special Municipalities

In 2010, Taiwan underwent administrative reorganization, merging and upgrading counties and cities to form six special municipalities: Taipei City, New Taipei City, Taoyuan City, Taichung City, Tainan City, and Kaohsiung City. This "Six Municipalities" framework officially established Taiwan's new urban development structure.

The upgrade wasn't merely administrative adjustment but reflected urban development realities. These six cities concentrate over 70% of Taiwan's population and over 80% of economic activities, creating unprecedented urban concentration.

## The Differentiated Development of Six Special Municipalities

### Taipei Metropolitan Area: Twin Cities at the Political-Economic Core

Taipei City and New Taipei City form Taiwan's largest metropolitan area with over 7 million people, serving as the island's political, economic, and cultural center. However, the two cities have vastly different development patterns.

Taipei City is a typical post-industrial city dominated by services. Financial, information technology, and cultural creative industries are highly concentrated here, attracting Taiwan's best talent. While housing prices are astronomical and living costs staggering, it provides the most job opportunities and development space.

New Taipei City plays the role of Taipei's "satellite city." Areas like Banqiao, Zhonghe, and Yonghe became residential areas for Taipei, while Xindian and Xizhi developed into technology parks. New Taipei's development completely depends on Taipei, creating a "live in New Taipei, work in Taipei" commuting pattern.

This twin-city development model has both advantages and problems. The advantage is reducing pressure on Taipei while providing more living space; the problem is intensified transportation burden, with millions of daily cross-city commuters overwhelming the transportation system.

### Taoyuan: Rise of the Airport City

Taoyuan's rapid development is a pleasant surprise in Taiwan's urbanization. Originally just an agricultural county, Taoyuan's destiny completely changed with the international airport. The Aerotropolis plan, Taoyuan HSR station, and various technology parks made Taoyuan Taiwan's fastest-growing city.

Taoyuan's development model is unique—typical "airport economy." Logistics, aviation industry, and international business are highly developed, attracting substantial foreign investment. Meanwhile, due to relatively cheap land prices, it became the preferred destination for many Taipei residents.

However, Taoyuan faces problems of overly rapid development. Infrastructure can't keep up with population growth, causing severe traffic congestion. Urban planning lacks holistic thinking, with poor organic connections between new and old development areas.

### Taichung: Regional Capital of Central Taiwan

Taichung is Taiwan's second-largest city with 2.8 million people, the undisputed leader of central Taiwan. Taichung's development is relatively balanced, emphasizing both manufacturing and services as a typical regional center city.

Taichung's greatest advantage is its geographical location. Situated in central Taiwan, it's a necessary passage for north-south transportation and a gateway for east-west connections. The development of Taichung Port transformed the city from inland to coastal, strengthening external connections.

Precision machinery and optoelectronics are Taichung's strengths, forming important industrial clusters. Taichung is also an important cultural city with numerous historical sites and rich cultural activities. However, Taichung's development is still limited by scale, unable to compete with Taipei in high-end services.

### Kaohsiung: Transformation Challenges of an Industrial City

Kaohsiung is Taiwan's traditional industrial powerhouse, with heavy industries like petrochemicals, steel, and shipbuilding highly concentrated. However, with industrial restructuring, Kaohsiung faces severe transformation challenges.

Kaohsiung Port was once the world's third-largest container port, but its position gradually declined with the rise of mainland China's ports. Traditional industries have relocated, young populations have emigrated, leaving Kaohsiung in development difficulties. Recent years have seen the city government vigorously promoting industrial transformation, developing tourism, cultural creativity, and green energy industries with gradually visible results.

Kaohsiung's greatest assets are vast development space and complete infrastructure. Kaohsiung Port, Kaohsiung International Airport, and convenient transportation networks are all favorable conditions for transformation. However, how to reposition and find new development momentum remains a major challenge for Kaohsiung.

### Tainan: Modern Interpretation of Historical Culture

Tainan is Taiwan's oldest city with rich historical and cultural resources. But due to historical burdens, its development pace has been relatively slow. After Tainan County and City merger and upgrade, development space expanded, beginning to seek new development models.

Tainan's development strategy is "culture-based city building," actively developing cultural tourism. Meanwhile, leveraging Southern Taiwan Science Park's technological capabilities to develop high-tech industries. This "ancient capital, new appearance" development model has created a unique development path for Tainan.

However, Tainan also faces problems of population aging and industrial monotony. How to attract young talent while preserving historical culture is a key challenge for Tainan's development.

## Non-Six Municipalities: The Crisis of Marginalization

### Eastern Counties: Beautiful Periphery

Hualien and Taitung, despite their stunning natural landscapes, have relatively backward economic development due to remote locations and inconvenient transportation. These two counties face continuous population loss and serious aging, forming a "beautiful periphery."

Eastern development difficulties mainly stem from transportation barriers. The Central Mountain Range blocks east-west transportation, and the Suhua Highway and South Link Highway are treacherous mountain roads limiting people and goods flow. Although railways connect the region, they are slow with infrequent services, unable to support large-scale economic activities.

Tourism is the most important industry in eastern Taiwan, but it's highly seasonal with limited employment opportunities. While agriculture is high quality, it's small scale and cannot support the overall economy. How to develop sustainable economy while protecting natural environment remains an important issue for eastern Taiwan.

### Central and Southern Counties: Industrial Transformation Struggles

Traditional agricultural counties like Changhua, Yunlin, Chiayi, and Pingtung face enormous pressure from industrial transformation. With agriculture declining, limited industrial development, and massive youth emigration, they experience "rural hollowing."

These counties' difficulties stem from lacking development engines. Without major ports, international airports, or major construction projects, attracting investment is difficult. While traditional agriculture is important, its limited output value cannot support modern economy.

Recently, these counties have begun seeking new development paths, developing precision agriculture, leisure tourism, and green energy industries, attempting to find new development momentum. However, the transformation process is difficult and requires long-term effort.

### Offshore Islands: Special Development Challenges

Offshore areas like Penghu, Kinmen, and Matsu face more special development challenges. With remote locations, small populations, weak economic foundations, and limited development choices.

Penghu relies mainly on tourism but is heavily affected by seasonality and weather. Kinmen and Matsu, due to their fortress status, face many development restrictions. Although actively developing tourism in recent years, scale remains limited.

Island development needs more central government support, including transportation subsidies, tax incentives, and special policies. How to improve development levels while maintaining uniqueness is an important issue for offshore islands.

## Multiple Dimensions of Rural-Urban Disparity

### Economic Development Gaps

Economic development gaps between Taiwan's urban and rural areas are obvious. The six special municipalities concentrate over 80% of Taiwan's corporate headquarters and over 70% of employment opportunities, with per capita income far exceeding non-six municipality areas.

According to statistics, Taipei City's per capita income exceeds NT$1 million, the highest in Taiwan; conversely, agricultural counties like Yunlin, Chiayi, and Pingtung have per capita income below NT$600,000, a gap exceeding 1.5 times.

This economic disparity reflects not only income but also employment opportunities. High-paying, high-skill jobs concentrate in major cities, while rural areas mostly offer low-paying, low-skill jobs. Young people must leave their hometowns for cities to pursue better development opportunities.

### Unequal Public Services

Quality gaps in public services between urban and rural areas are also obvious. Urban areas have more and better schools, hospitals, and cultural facilities, while rural areas relatively lack these.

Educational resource rural-urban gaps are particularly serious. Elite high schools and quality universities concentrate in cities; rural students often need to leave home for good education. This educational resource inequality exacerbates social mobility difficulties.

Healthcare resource gaps are even more serious. Large hospitals and specialist doctors concentrate in cities, while rural areas severely lack medical resources. Rural residents often need to cross counties for specialist care, causing great inconvenience.

### New Challenges of Digital Divide

With the advent of digital society, the digital divide has become a new dimension of rural-urban disparity. Urban areas have complete internet infrastructure and rich digital services; rural areas are relatively backward.

This digital divide affects not only life convenience but also economic development opportunities. E-commerce, remote work, and online education all require good internet infrastructure. Rural areas lacking digital infrastructure find it harder to participate in the new economy.

The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted the severity of the digital divide. Remote learning and working from home became routine, but students and workers in rural areas often struggled to adapt to new work and learning modes due to insufficient equipment and unstable internet.

## Urban Renewal: Challenges and Opportunities

### Rebirth of Old Urban Areas

Many of Taiwan's city centers face aging problems. Old buildings, insufficient infrastructure, and population outflow create urban decay phenomena. Urban renewal has become an important policy tool to solve these problems.

Taipei City's urban renewal is most aggressive, using floor area ratio incentives and tax preferences to encourage old building reconstruction. The successful development of Xinyi District became an urban renewal model. However, urban renewal has also caused many controversies—complex land ownership, divergent resident opinions, and lengthy procedures make urban renewal difficult to promote.

Other cities are also promoting urban renewal with varying results. Taichung's Seventh Redevelopment District and Kaohsiung's Asia New Bay Area are important urban renewal cases. However, how to conduct urban renewal while preserving historical culture remains a challenge for all cities.

### New Thinking in Social Housing

Facing high housing prices, social housing has become important government policy. Through construction or rent subsidies, providing affordable housing to those in need. This not only solves housing problems but also promotes social integration.

Taipei City's social housing policy is most aggressive, completing multiple social housing projects. However, social housing faces "NIMBY" problems, with many people worried that social housing affects surrounding property values, creating resistance.

How to integrate social housing into communities and avoid creating new ghettos is key to policy success. Combining social services, cultural activities, and industrial development creates multiple functions, making social housing a positive community force.

### Future Vision of Smart Cities

Facing urbanization challenges, smart cities have become a new development direction. Through IoT, big data, and artificial intelligence, improving urban management efficiency and citizen quality of life.

Cities like Taipei, Taichung, and Kaohsiung are promoting smart city plans. Smart transportation, smart energy, and smart governance applications are gradually becoming popular. However, smart cities are not just technical issues but governance philosophy transformation, requiring government, business, and citizen participation.

## Strategies for Reducing Rural-Urban Disparity

### Balanced Regional Development

The government promotes "balanced regional development" policies, reducing rural-urban disparities through forward-looking infrastructure and local revitalization plans. Improving transportation infrastructure, developing local characteristic industries, and enhancing public service quality are all important strategies.

However, balanced regional development cannot rely solely on government; market mechanisms must cooperate. Creating incentives for businesses to invest in rural areas and attracting talent back to start businesses can form sustainable development momentum.

### Digital Opportunity Equality

Reducing the digital divide is an important issue for balanced urban-rural development. The government promotes "digital opportunity equality" plans, improving internet infrastructure in rural areas and providing digital skills training.

5G network popularization brings new opportunities for rural areas. Through high-speed internet, rural areas can develop remote services and e-commerce. Digital nomadism and remote work allow people to work without being confined to cities.

### New Models of Local Revitalization

Local revitalization is an important recent policy, revitalizing rural economies by discovering local characteristics, innovating business models, and attracting young people to return. Many successful cases prove that rural areas can find new development momentum.

However, local revitalization requires long-term cultivation and cannot be rushed. It needs deep understanding of local culture, combining local resources, and cultivating local talent to establish sustainable development models.

## Vision for Sustainable Urban Development

Taiwan's urbanization has entered a mature stage; future focus should not be continued expansion but quality improvement. Building livable cities, developing circular economy, and achieving carbon neutrality are all important goals.

At the same time, we need to rethink urban-rural relationships. Not to eliminate all disparities, but to build complementary relationships. Cities provide employment opportunities and public services, rural areas provide food security and ecological services, forming symbiotic and mutually prosperous relationships.

Although Taiwan is small, its geographical environment is diverse with rich development potential. Through appropriate policy guidance and market mechanisms, we believe Taiwan can create an urban-rural development pattern that is both efficient and equitable, both modern and sustainable.
