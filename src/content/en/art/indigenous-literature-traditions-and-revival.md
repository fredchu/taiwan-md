# Indigenous Literature - Traditions and Revival

In Taiwan's literary landscape, there flows an invisible yet most ancient bloodline—the indigenous literary tradition that has been coursing since time immemorial. It exists without written script yet runs deeper than any text; it has no books yet proves richer than any library. This is a living literature, a poetic expression intimately bound with life, land, and ancestral spirits.

When we discuss Taiwan's literary history, to overlook indigenous literature would be like trying to understand a great tree while ignoring its root system. Indigenous literature is the matrix of Taiwanese literature, the wellspring from which all subsequent literary forms emerge. It not only witnesses the most ancient memories of this land but also finds new life in the pens of contemporary indigenous writers, becoming a vital force against cultural amnesia.

## Oral Literature: The Universe in Voice

### Mythology: The Genesis of Cosmos

Taiwan's indigenous mythology is extraordinarily rich, with each tribe possessing its unique creation myths. These myths are not merely literature but comprehensive systems encompassing philosophy, religion, ethics, and scientific knowledge.

In Atayal creation mythology, humans are born from a gigantic rock. This seemingly simple story actually contains profound life philosophy: the inseparable bond between humans and earth, the resilience and eternity of life. Meanwhile, Bunun creation myths describe how humans descended from heaven and learned to use fire—a metaphor for the dawn of human civilization.

The Paiwan legend of "Princess Ba-leng," which tells of a princess's love story with a snake prince, has become one of Taiwan's most famous indigenous love legends. In this tale, the complex relationships between humans and nature, civilization and wilderness, love and responsibility receive poetic expression.

### Legends: History as Poetry

Indigenous legends often represent poetic treatments of historical events. The Tsou legend of "War God and Peace God" reflects the history of tribal warfare and reconciliation; the Puyuma "Moon Stone" legend records the trajectory of tribal migration.

The distinctive feature of these legends lies in their difference from Han historical scholarship's pursuit of objective historical facts. Instead, they emphasize the spiritual essence of historical events. A tribal war might be remembered as a struggle between deities; a natural disaster might be interpreted as an ancestral warning. This "poetic historiography" embodies the indigenous peoples' unique historical and worldview.

### Songs: The Rhythm of Life

Indigenous songs are the literary form most closely connected to daily life, covering nearly every aspect of existence: hunting songs, farming songs, weaving songs, wedding songs, funeral songs, and ceremonial songs.

The Bunun "eight-part harmony" is a treasure of Taiwan's indigenous musical culture. This complex harmonic form not only achieves the highest artistic standards musically but also carries profound literary significance. Though the lyrics are concise, their repetitive chanting effect creates a sacred poetic atmosphere.

The Amis "Sea Ceremony Songs" embody the maritime people's boldness and poetry. These songs not only depict the ocean's magnificence but also express deep contemplation on the cycle of life and death. "Waves come and go, people come and depart, only the ocean endures"—this reflection on eternity and impermanence carries strong philosophical undertones.

### Ceremonial Literature: Sacred Verses

Indigenous ceremonies are important venues for literary performance. In these sacred rituals, prayers, incantations, and invocations form a rich ceremonial literature.

The Tsou "War Ceremony," Bunun "Ear-shooting Ceremony," and Paiwan "Five-year Ceremony" all have corresponding ceremonial literature. These literary works are characterized by their sacred and symbolic language, often employing ancient vocabulary and elegant syntax to create a solemn, dignified atmosphere.

## Modern Indigenous Literature: Revival of Tradition

### Background of Literary Renaissance

After Taiwan's martial law ended in the 1980s, the indigenous rights movement emerged, and indigenous peoples began reclaiming their cultural identity. In this process, literature became an important tool for cultural revival. A group of indigenous writers began using modern literary forms to rewrite indigenous history, culture, and contemporary circumstances.

This "cultural revival" was not simple restoration but rather reinterpretation and development of indigenous culture within modern civilization's context. These writers faced dual challenges: maintaining indigenous culture's uniqueness while engaging in dialogue with modern civilization.

### Sun Ta-chuan: Theoretical Architect of Indigenous Literature

Sun Ta-chuan (Puyuma, Paelabang) is a major theorist and practitioner of contemporary indigenous literature. His essay collection "Mountain and Ocean World" established theoretical foundations for indigenous literature. In his view, the core quality of indigenous literature is the "mountain-ocean spirit"—possessing both the mountain's steadfastness and sublimity, and the ocean's tolerance and fluidity.

Sun Ta-chuan's creative style combines poetry and rationality. He can describe indigenous cultural landscapes with beautiful prose while analyzing indigenous circumstances in modern society with profound contemplation. His works like "Thinking with Feet" and "Ethnic Construction in the Cracks" opened discursive space for indigenous literature.

### Walesi Norgan: Tribal Soul in Poetry

Walesi Norgan (Atayal) is a representative figure among contemporary indigenous poets. His poetry possesses strong ethnic consciousness and modernity. In his works, traditional Atayal culture and modern life experiences interweave to create unique poetic aesthetics.

His masterpiece "Ino's Re-survey" pays tribute to Japanese colonial period anthropologist Ino Kanori while reflecting on the history of indigenous peoples being studied and interpreted. This poem rewrites indigenous history in modern language, demonstrating indigenous agency and dignity.

Walesi Norgan's poetic language is richly musical, continuous with the characteristics of indigenous oral literature. He excels at using imagery and symbolism to transform abstract ethnic emotions into concrete, tangible poetic language.

### Syaman Rapongan: Pioneer of Ocean Literature

Syaman Rapongan (Tao) is an important writer in Taiwan's maritime literature. As a member of Orchid Island's Tao people, his works are deeply rooted in oceanic cultural soil. His essays "Eyes of the Sky" and "Black Wings" describe Tao maritime life with poetic language, revealing the profound relationship between humans and the ocean.

Syaman Rapongan's writing possesses unique "oceanic consciousness." For him, the ocean is not merely where Tao people live but their spiritual homeland. His works overflow with reverence and praise for the ocean while reflecting modern civilization's impact on traditional maritime culture.

His literary language carries strong vernacular coloring, sourced from the Tao people's rich oral literature tradition. He excels at employing Tao thinking patterns and expressive habits to create modern essays with ethnic characteristics.

### Badai: Historical Writing and Cultural Memory

Badai (Puyuma) is an important representative of contemporary indigenous novelists. His novel "The Stork" uses Puyuma history as background to describe indigenous experiences during the Japanese colonial period. This work is not only literary creation but also a rewriting of forgotten history.

Badai's creative characteristic is combining historical and realistic sensibilities. His works often use historical events as background while focusing on ordinary people's destinies and emotions. He excels at reflecting entire ethnic histories through individual stories, embodying indigenous literature's quality of "seeing the big through the small."

His other important work "Iron Horse" focuses on contemporary indigenous living conditions. In this novel, conflicts between traditional culture and modern civilization, contrasts between city and tribe, and personal identity confusion receive profound exploration.

## Aesthetic Qualities of Indigenous Literature

### Inheritance of Poetic Language

The most distinctive feature of indigenous literature is its rich poetic coloring. This poetry appears not only in verse but also permeates prose and fiction. This stems from the oral literature tradition—in ages without writing, poetic language was easier to remember and transmit.

Modern indigenous writers inherit this poetic tradition while combining modern literary techniques to create unique literary language. This language often possesses musicality, symbolism, and metaphorical quality, capable of creating rich imagery and profound artistic conception.

### Modern Transformation of Mythological Thinking

Another important quality of indigenous literature is the application of mythological thinking. In indigenous worldviews, the real world and mythological world are connected; there are no absolute boundaries between spirits and humans, nature and culture.

Modern indigenous writers transform this mythological thinking into modern literary techniques, creating works with magical coloring. In these works, reality and fantasy, history and myth, personal experience and collective memory interweave to form unique narrative aesthetics.

### Deep Expression of Land Consciousness

The core theme of indigenous literature is the relationship between humans and land. In indigenous cultural concepts, land is not only the foundation of survival but also spiritual dependence. This profound land consciousness receives full expression in modern indigenous literature.

Modern indigenous writers often center on land to unfold thinking about history, culture, and identity. Their works overflow with attachment to homeland landscapes, concern about land loss, and reflection on environmental destruction. This land consciousness not only enriches Taiwan literature's spiritual content but also provides important ideological resources for contemporary environmental literature.

## Contemporary Significance of Indigenous Literature

### Preservation of Cultural Diversity

Against the backdrop of globalization, indigenous literature's existence carries important cultural significance. It not only preserves Taiwan's most ancient cultural memories but also provides different thinking methods and expressive forms for contemporary literature.

Indigenous literature's diversity manifests across language, content, and form. Each tribe's literature has its own characteristics; this cultural diversity is humanity's precious civilizational treasure that requires careful protection and transmission.

### Ecological Civilization Insights

Indigenous natural philosophy provides important insights for contemporary environmental issues. In indigenous culture, humans and nature exist in harmonious symbiosis; this ecological wisdom offers vital ideological resources for solving contemporary environmental crises.

The rich nature writing in indigenous literature possesses not only literary value but also ecological value. It reminds us to reconsider human-nature relationships and seek sustainable development paths.

### Reconstruction of Cultural Identity

For indigenous peoples, literature is an important pathway for reconstructing cultural identity. Under long-term cultural oppression and assimilation policies, many indigenous people lost identification with their ethnic cultures. The rise of modern indigenous literature provides opportunities for indigenous peoples to rediscover and identify with their cultures.

Simultaneously, indigenous literature promotes understanding and dialogue between different ethnic groups. It allows non-indigenous readers opportunities to understand indigenous history and culture, promoting Taiwan society's pluralistic development.

## Conclusion: Eternal Songs

From ancient myths and legends to contemporary literary creation, indigenous literature flows like an endless song, resonating across Taiwan's land for thousands of years. This song sometimes soars high and stirring, expressing longing for freedom; sometimes meanders distant and deep, chanting attachment to homeland; sometimes resonates profound and weighty, bearing history's burden.

In contemporary Taiwan's multicultural landscape, indigenous literature plays an irreplaceable role. It serves as both historical witness and cultural transmitter; both reality critic and future inspirer. It reminds us not to forget the most ancient, precious cultural heritage while pursuing modernization.

As Walesi Norgan writes in poetry: "The songs of ancestors flow through my veins." This song transcends time and space, connecting ancient and modern; it will continue resonating in Taiwan's literary sky, adding the most primitive, purest colors to this land's literary garden.
