# Taiwanese Traditional Arts: Folk Treasures

Taiwanese traditional arts serve as living vessels of the island's cultural memory, carrying the collective emotions and beliefs of immigrant society. Amid globalization's waves, these folk treasures not only maintain their vitality but also demonstrate remarkable adaptability and creativity in the modernization process.

## The Golden Age and Modern Transformation of Budaixi (Glove Puppetry)

### Huang Jun-xiong and the Rise of Television Puppetry

In 1970, when Huang Jun-xiong's *The Legend of the Righteous Hero* first aired on TTV, no one anticipated it would become a miracle in Taiwan's television history. The character Shi Yan-wen not only achieved an astounding 97% viewership rating but drove the entire Taiwan society into a frenzy. Huang Jun-xiong moved traditional puppet theater from theater venues onto television screens, elevating puppet shows from folk entertainment to national pastime through exquisite puppet craftsmanship, innovative sound and lighting effects, and music.

This success was no accident. While Huang Jun-xiong inherited his father Huang Hai-dai's skills, more importantly, he possessed keen insight into modern media. Understanding television's unique characteristics as a new medium, he adjusted narrative pacing and enhanced visual effects, helping traditional arts find their place in the new era.

### Political Intervention and Artistic Destiny

However, in 1974, the KMT government banned all television puppet shows under the pretext of "promoting Mandarin" and "disrupting normal agricultural and industrial schedules." This prohibition was not merely language policy but suppression of Taiwan's indigenous culture. Puppet theater was forced to halt at its most glorious moment, and the ten-year void nearly lost an entire generation of audiences for this art form.

This history reminds us that traditional arts' survival often intertwines closely with political environments. Cultural policies are not merely administrative decisions but directly affect art forms' life and death.

### Pili's Modernization Revolution

After puppet theater regained freedom in the 1980s, Huang Jun-xiong's sons Huang Qiang-hua and Huang Wen-ze established Pili International Multimedia, pioneering an entirely new business model. Rather than relying on television broadcast, they produced videotapes for direct sale, establishing puppet theater's independent kingdom. The 1995 launch of Pili Satellite TV gave puppet shows their dedicated broadcast platform.

Pili puppet theater's success lies in its modernization strategy: sophisticated special effects production, complex world-building, and commercialized management models. Characters like Su Huan-jen and Ye Xiao-chai became not just dramatic figures but cultural symbols and commercial brands. This transformation evolved puppet theater from traditional folk opera to modern entertainment industry, proving traditional arts' survival possibilities in commercial society.

## Gezaixi (Taiwanese Opera) and Indigenous Aesthetics

### From Folk to Stage

Gezaixi is the only traditional opera form entirely nurtured on Taiwan's soil. Originating in Yilan, it fused folk musical elements like gezai tunes, four-line verses, and seven-character melodies, gradually forming a complete dramatic system in the early 20th century. Gezaixi's precious quality lies in its "wild nature"—without court background or literati packaging, it emerged purely from common people's daily life.

### Ming Hua Yuan's Family Heritage

Ming Hua Yuan Opera Troupe represents another modernization path for Gezaixi. Unlike Pili's commercialization, Ming Hua Yuan chose artistic refinement. They maintained Gezaixi's traditional characteristics while pursuing higher artistic standards in stage design, musical arrangement, and script creation.

Ming Hua Yuan's success stems from establishing a complete talent cultivation system. From founding brothers Chen Sheng-fu and Chen Sheng-zai to second and third-generation successors, Ming Hua Yuan demonstrates family inheritance's importance in traditional arts preservation. They transmit not just techniques but also artistic dedication and passion.

### Tang Mei-yun's Refinement Exploration

Tang Mei-yun Taiwanese Opera Troupe represents Gezaixi's refined development. Having emerged from television Gezaixi's golden age and experienced various developmental stages—indoor theaters, outdoor stages, and television—Tang Mei-yun chose the "inherit tradition, create innovation" path when founding her troupe in 1997.

Tang Mei-yun's contribution lies in elevating Gezaixi to artistic theater levels. Through collaboration with modern theater practitioners, she endowed Gezaixi with richer expressive techniques while maintaining its unique musicality and emotional expression. Her troupe's selection as a "Taiwan Brand Team" by the Ministry of Culture proves the refinement path's success.

## Musical Traditions: The Elegant-Popular Distinction in Beiguan and Nanguan

Taiwan's traditional music preserves rich Fujian cultural heritage. Beiguan music features strong rhythms and passionate emotions, commonly used in temple festivals and opera accompaniment; Nanguan music is elegant and delicate, called a "living musical fossil" that maintains ancient Central Plains music's original features.

This coexistence of elegant and popular musical ecology reflects Taiwan society's complexity. Beiguan satisfies folk entertainment needs, while Nanguan carries cultural spiritual pursuits. Both coexist harmoniously, together constituting Taiwan's complete musical cultural landscape.

## Religious Arts: Eight Generals and Parade Culture

The Eight Generals (*Ba Jia Jiang*) represent Taiwan's most visually striking performance art in religious culture. These performers, dressed in gorgeous costumes with elaborate facial paint, serve as divine protectors in temple festivals, driving away evil through specific steps and movements. The Eight Generals' aesthetics embody wild sacredness, fusing religious belief, theatrical performance, and social ritual.

Parade culture (*zhentou*) further displays Taiwan folk arts' diversity. From dragon and lion dances to Song Dynasty formations, from zodiac performances to eight-instrument ensembles, each parade type has specific functions and aesthetics. These performances are not merely entertainment but symbols of community identity and cultural expression of traditional social organization.

## Craft Arts: Jiaozhi Ceramics and Cut-and-Paste Building Poetry

Decorative arts on Taiwan's traditional architecture reached extremely high standards. Jiaozhi ceramics, with their bright colors and exquisite forms, transform mythological stories and historical allusions into three-dimensional visual narratives. Cut-and-paste techniques (*jianzhan*) use various colored porcelain pieces to create beautiful patterns, filling building surfaces with rich textural variations.

These crafts are not mere decoration but cultural creations by artisans. Each work carries folk wisdom and aesthetic concepts, demonstrating craftsmen's artistic creativity. Under modernization's impact, these skills face extinction risks, yet increasing numbers of artists invest in preservation and innovation work.

## Wood Carving Arts: From Utility to Aesthetics

Taiwan's wood carving craftsmanship has deep roots, from temple dragon pillars to furniture decorations, from deity statue creation to architectural ornamentation—wood carving permeates every life corner. Traditional wood carving emphasizes "three parts carving, seven parts polishing," focusing on line fluidity and form harmony.

Modern wood carving art, while maintaining traditional techniques, also explores new expressive languages. Artists no longer limit themselves to traditional subjects, beginning to express modern people's emotions and thoughts through wood carving, revitalizing this ancient craft.

## Modern Challenges and Opportunities for Traditional Arts

Taiwan's traditional arts face dual challenges in the modernization process: maintaining cultural characteristics while adapting to modern lifestyle patterns. Successful examples like Pili puppet theater and Ming Hua Yuan found balance between tradition and modernity.

More importantly, these art forms prove that cultural vitality lies not in unchanged preservation but in innovative advancement with the times. Contemporary Taiwan's traditional arts are writing an exciting chapter about cultural inheritance and innovation, providing the world with a successful cultural transformation model.

The value of Taiwan's traditional arts lies not only in historical significance but also in their contributions to contemporary cultural construction. In the globalization era, these folk treasures remind us that true cultural power comes from creative inheritance rooted in the land.
