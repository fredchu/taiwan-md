# Taiwan Marine Ecology and Coral Reef Conservation

Taiwan, located in the Western Pacific, encompasses marine areas of 170,000 square kilometers — 4.7 times its terrestrial area. The Kuroshio Current and Chinese Coastal Current converge here, creating rich and diverse marine ecosystems. From tropical coral reefs to temperate rocky reefs, Taiwan's waters nurture over 3,000 fish species, 1,000 mollusk species, and 32 cetacean species, ranking among the world's top biodiversity regions.

## Characteristics of Taiwan's Marine Ecology

### Geographic Location Uniqueness

The Taiwan Strait averages 60 meters in depth with maximum depth of 184 meters, representing typical continental shelf shallow seas. Eastern waters, however, constitute Pacific deep seas, with 3,000-meter-deep oceanic trenches adjacent to Taiwan's main island. This "shallow sea on one side, deep sea on the other" topography creates Taiwan's marine ecological distinctiveness.

The Kuroshio Current serves as Taiwan's marine ecological lifeline. This warm current from the Philippine Sea brings tropical marine organisms while providing abundant nutrients. The Kuroshio flows along Taiwan's eastern coast at speeds of 1-1.5 meters per second, ranking among the world's strongest currents. Each spring and summer, Kuroshio branches enter the Taiwan Strait, forming "Kuroshio subdivisions."

### Marine Ecosystem Types

Taiwan's waters encompass multiple marine ecosystems:

**Coral reef ecosystems** distribute across Kenting, Green Island, Orchid Island, Xiaoliuqiu, and Penghu. Taiwan's reef-building corals include 60 genera and 240 species, representing one-third of the world's reef-building coral species. Soft coral diversity proves even richer, with 130 species in Kenting waters alone.

**Rocky reef ecosystems** primarily distribute in northern and northeastern coastal waters. Rocky reef formations at Yehliu, Longdong, and Bitou Cape provide diversified habitat spaces. Intertidal organisms prove abundant, including sea anemones, sea urchins, barnacles, and gastropods.

**Sandy-muddy bottom ecosystems** distribute along western coasts. Despite lower species diversity, biomass remains enormous. Clams, oysters, crabs, and other economically valuable shellfish abound, representing important fishery resources.

**Deep sea ecosystems** distribute in eastern offshore waters. Areas exceeding 200 meters depth contain specialized deep-sea fish, cephalopods, and crustaceans. Recent discoveries of many new deep-sea species demonstrate Taiwan's deep-sea ecological research potential.

### Marine Protected Area System

Taiwan established four marine national parks: Kenting, Dongsha Atoll, Penghu South Four Islands, and Taijiang, totaling approximately 350,000 hectares. Dongsha Atoll National Park covers the largest area at 354,000 hectares.

Beyond national parks, 26 local government-designated marine protected areas (fishery resource conservation areas) total approximately 56,000 hectares. These protected areas prohibit or restrict fishing activities, providing marine ecosystems respite.

## Current Status of Coral Reef Ecosystems

### Coral Reef Distribution and Characteristics

Taiwan's coral reefs primarily distribute across southern regions and offshore islands. Kenting National Park contains the largest fringing reef area with coral coverage of approximately 30-50%. Green Island and Orchid Island maintain better-preserved coral reefs with coverage reaching 60-80%. Xiaoliuqiu, despite small size, contains rich coral species as Taiwan's main island's only coral reef island.

Penghu's coral reefs feature table corals as characteristics. Due to more northern latitude, only cold-tolerant coral species survive. Dongsha Atoll represents Taiwan's only atoll with coral coverage reaching 70%, earning the title "Pearl of the South China Sea."

### Coral Bleaching Crisis

Coral bleaching represents the greatest threat facing Taiwan's coral reefs. Rising seawater temperatures constitute the primary cause. Coral symbiotic algae cannot withstand high temperatures, leaving coral tissues and causing corals to lose coloration and nutritional sources.

2020 marked the most severe bleaching year in Taiwan's coral reef history. Summer seawater temperatures consistently exceeded 29°C, causing massive bleaching at Kenting, Green Island, and Orchid Island. Kenting's Wanliton coral reef experienced 90% bleaching rates, while Green Island's Shilang coral reef exceeded 70% bleaching.

Long-term monitoring data from Academia Sinica's Biodiversity Research Center shows Taiwan's coral reef live coral coverage declined approximately 50% over the past 30 years — from average 60% in the 1980s to current levels around 30%.

### Other Threats Facing Coral Reefs

**Ocean acidification** serves as coral reefs' invisible killer. Increasing atmospheric carbon dioxide concentrations cause seawater to absorb CO2, forming carbonic acid and lowering seawater pH. Acidified seawater affects coral skeleton calcification processes, slowing coral growth.

**Pollutants** include agricultural fertilizers, domestic sewage, and industrial wastewater. Excessive nitrogen and phosphorus nutrients trigger algal blooms competing with corals for living space. Chemical components in sunscreen products also prove harmful to coral health.

**Physical destruction** stems from diving activities, fishing operations, and vessel groundings. Inappropriate diving behavior tramples corals, bottom trawling scrapes seafloors, and ship propellers cut coral branches. Kenting region receives approximately 500,000 annual water activity participants, creating enormous management pressure.

**Crown-of-thorns starfish outbreaks** represent cyclical threats. These coral-eating starfish reproduce massively when natural enemies decrease. The 2015-2016 crown-of-thorns starfish outbreak in Kenting waters prompted large-scale removal operations by volunteers and divers, removing approximately 30,000 crown-of-thorns starfish.

## Coral Restoration and Protection Actions

### Coral Restoration Technology Development

Taiwan's coral restoration technology leads the Asia-Pacific region. Academia Sinica's developed "coral nursery" technique first establishes coral cultivation sites in ocean waters, then transplants grown corals to degraded reef areas. This method achieves survival rates of 70-80%.

The National Museum of Marine Science and Technology promotes "Coral Conservation Action Plans," combining scientific research, education promotion, and community participation. They developed "coral fragmentation propagation techniques," cultivating healthy coral small fragments into new individuals.

The Australian Institute of Marine Science (AIMS) collaborates with Taiwan introducing "coral assisted evolution" techniques. Through temperature acclimatization and probiotic cultivation, they enhance coral high-temperature tolerance. These "enhanced corals" withstand temperatures 2-3°C higher than ordinary corals.

### Citizen Science Participation

"Taiwan Coral Reef Check" represents Asia's largest coral reef citizen science project. Since 2009, over 500 volunteers annually participate in coral reef surveys. Participants include divers, ocean enthusiasts, and school teachers and students conducting standardized surveys after training.

Survey results show that better-protected areas (such as marine protected areas and national parks) maintain significantly better coral conditions. For example, Green Island's "Da Bai Sha" no-take zone maintains live coral coverage above 60%.

### Legal Protection Mechanisms

The "Cultural Heritage Preservation Act" designates special coral reefs as natural monuments. Kenting's "Coral Reefs in Silver Wattle Invasion Areas" and Green Island's "Chaikou Weather Station Coral Reefs" receive legal protection.

The "Marine Pollution Prevention Act" prohibits vessels from arbitrarily deploying anchors that damage coral reefs. National Park laws prohibit any interference in core protection zones. Local governments also establish diving management regulations limiting simultaneous diver numbers.

## Current Status and Challenges of Cetacean Conservation

### Taiwan Waters Cetacean Diversity

Taiwan's waters record 32 cetacean species, representing 37% of global cetacean species. These include killer whales, sperm whales, false killer whales, spinner dolphins, bottlenose dolphins, and Indo-Pacific humpback dolphins. Eastern waters, adjacent to deep seas, host more large whales; western waters predominantly feature small cetaceans.

The Taiwan humpback dolphin (Indo-Pacific humpback dolphin Taiwan subspecies) represents Taiwan's endemic subspecies and most endangered marine mammal. Approximately 50 individuals remain, distributed along western coasts from Miaoli to Tainan. Due to habitat destruction, fishing bycatch, pollution, and other threats, the International Union for Conservation of Nature lists it as critically endangered.

### Threats Facing Cetaceans

**Ship strikes** represent the primary threat to large cetaceans. International commercial shipping lanes off Hualien and Taitung frequently witness whale-ship collisions. From 2019-2021, Taiwan's eastern coast experienced 37 cetacean stranding incidents, with 13 cases suspected of ship strikes.

**Fishing bycatch** annually causes hundreds of cetacean deaths. Gillnets, seine nets, and other fishing gear entangle cetaceans, causing drowning. Indo-Pacific humpback dolphins prove particularly susceptible to gillnet bycatch, representing the primary cause of population decline.

**Noise pollution** affects cetacean sonar systems. Ship engines, marine construction, and military activities generate noise interfering with cetacean communication and foraging. Deep-diving species like sperm whales prove particularly sensitive to noise.

**Habitat destruction** includes coastal development, land reclamation, and offshore wind power construction. Taiwan humpback dolphin habitats overlap with western industrial zones, facing severe habitat loss problems.

### Cetacean Conservation Actions

**The Formosa Cetacean Research and Conservation Group** serves as Taiwan's most important cetacean conservation organization, established in 1999. They promote cetacean rescue, ecological surveys, education advocacy, and policy initiatives, annually handling approximately 100-150 cetacean stranding rescue cases.

**The Taiwan White Dolphin Conservation Action Network** represents a specialized alliance protecting Taiwan humpback dolphins. They monitor humpback dolphin population dynamics, promote friendly fishing methods, and advocate marine protected area establishment. Currently campaigning to designate Taiwan humpback dolphin critical habitats as important wildlife habitats.

**Whale watching industry transformation** combines conservation with tourism. Hualien and Taitung whale watching operators cooperate with scientific surveys, collecting cetacean sighting data while establishing whale watching guidelines maintaining safe distances to avoid disturbing cetacean behavior.

### Scientific Research Progress

**Satellite tagging tracking** technology enables scientists to monitor cetacean movement paths in real time. Academia Sinica's Biodiversity Research Center uses satellite tags to track sperm whales off Hualien, discovering they dive to 2,000-meter depths for foraging.

**Acoustic monitoring** utilizes seafloor hydrophones to record cetacean sounds. Different species produce different call frequencies, enabling species identification and population estimation. Eastern waters acoustic monitoring networks reveal obvious seasonal variations in cetacean activities.

**Genetic analysis** helps clarify cetacean population structures and phylogenetic relationships. Taiwan humpback dolphin genetic research shows they have differentiated from mainland China's Indo-Pacific humpback dolphins into separate populations, supporting independent subspecies classification status.

## Marine National Park Protection Effectiveness

### Dongsha Atoll National Park

Dongsha Atoll National Park, established in 2007, represents Taiwan's first marine national park. The park covers 354,000 hectares with 99.9% marine areas. Strict protection measures maintain Dongsha coral reefs in excellent condition with live coral coverage above 70%.

The park records 615 fish species, 286 coral species, and 405 mollusk species. Large fish like humphead wrasse, sharks, and rays thrive here. 2020 marked the first recorded observation of manta ray aggregation and breeding behavior at Dongsha Atoll.

However, Dongsha faces challenges. Chinese fishing vessel illegal border crossings and coral collection activities occur frequently. Climate change-induced sea temperature rises also cause Dongsha coral bleaching phenomena.

### Penghu South Four Islands National Park

Penghu South Four Islands National Park, established in 2014, includes Dongyu Islet, Xiyu Islet, Dongji Islet, Xiji Islet, and surrounding waters. The park emphasizes "harmonious coexistence between humans and nature," allowing local residents traditional fishing activities.

Park coral reefs feature table corals adapting to Penghu's harsh marine environment. Terns represent park star species, with large numbers breeding here from April to September annually. Dongji Islet's tern breeding numbers can exceed 10,000 individuals.

### Protected Area Management Challenges

**Insufficient manpower** represents a common problem for marine protected areas. Marine patrols require vessels, equipment, and professional personnel with costs far exceeding terrestrial protected areas. Kenting National Park's marine area covers approximately 15,000 hectares but employs only 10 marine patrol officers.

**Enforcement difficulties** stem from marine openness. Illegal fishing, coral poaching, and illegal diving activities prove difficult to detect and prevent immediately. Recent introductions of unmanned aircraft, underwater cameras, and other technological equipment enhance patrol efficiency.

**Community participation** represents the key to long-term protection. Green Island, Orchid Island, and Xiaoliuqiu develop ecotourism, enabling local residents to gain economic benefits from conservation. However, excessive tourism may also create new environmental pressures.

## Future Prospects for Marine Conservation

### Marine Spatial Planning

Taiwan promotes the "National Ocean Policy White Paper," establishing marine spatial planning mechanisms. Marine areas are functionally categorized as conservation zones, sustainable use zones, and multi-objective use zones. The goal aims for marine protected area coverage reaching 30% of total marine area by 2030.

"Blue carbon" concepts receive increasing attention. Mangroves, seagrass beds, salt marshes, and other marine ecosystems possess strong carbon sequestration abilities. Protecting these "blue carbon ecosystems" simultaneously achieves biodiversity conservation and climate change mitigation goals.

### Technology Application Trends

**Environmental DNA (eDNA)** technology enables scientists to rapidly survey marine biodiversity. Simply collecting seawater samples and analyzing DNA fragments can identify species composition in waters. This method particularly suits surveying rare species or deep-sea organisms.

**Artificial intelligence** applies to marine ecological monitoring. AI can automatically identify fish and corals photographed by underwater cameras, dramatically improving survey efficiency. National Taiwan University collaborates with Microsoft developing "Marine AI," automatically analyzing coral reef ecosystem health conditions.

**Citizen science platforms** enable broader public participation in marine conservation. Mobile applications like "iNaturalist" allow divers, anglers, and beachgoers to contribute biological distribution data.

### International Cooperation Opportunities

Taiwan actively participates in international marine conservation networks, joining organizations like the "International Society for Reef Studies" and "International Whaling Commission," sharing conservation experiences and technologies.

Marine conservation cooperation with neighboring countries becomes increasingly important. Cetaceans, sea turtles, tunas, and other marine animals cross national boundaries requiring regional protection strategies. Taiwan maintains concrete cooperation projects with Japan and Philippines in cetacean conservation.

The "Indo-Pacific Regional Ocean Initiative" emphasizes marine sustainable development. Taiwan can deepen cooperation with Indo-Pacific nations in marine technology, conservation techniques, and sustainable fisheries.

Taiwan's marine conservation journey remains full of challenges but also hope. Through integrated efforts in scientific research, technological innovation, social participation, and international cooperation, Taiwan has opportunities to become an important Western Pacific marine conservation stronghold. Protecting marine ecology serves not only marine organisms but humanity's own future.
