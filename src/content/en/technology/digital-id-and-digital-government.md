# Digital ID and Digital Government

> **30-Second Overview:** In January 2021, a chip-based national ID card that was never issued cost the Taiwanese government roughly NT$280 million (US$9 million) in vendor compensation. The New eID project was supposed to let 23 million citizens access all government services with a single card — until more than 2,000 academics signed a petition against it and the plan was shelved. The irony: that same year, Taiwan's civic hacker community built a real-time mask availability map in days — a digital government service nobody was forced to use but everyone wanted. Taiwan's real digital governance story isn't about whether the technology works. It's about how you build trust.

---

## The Card That Triggered a Trust Crisis

In June 2019, Taiwan's Premier Su Tseng-chang posted a promotional video on Facebook for the upcoming national digital ID rollout. "In the past, a scholar could learn about the world without leaving home," he said. "In the future, a scholar will be able to handle all government affairs without leaving home." Cabinet Secretary-General Li Meng-yen added: "128 countries already use chip-based digital IDs. Taiwan needs to catch up." (As reported by The Reporter, 2021)

What the Cabinet didn't anticipate was backlash on multiple fronts. The Taiwan Association for Human Rights and the Open Culture Foundation launched petitions. The Judicial Reform Foundation filed an administrative lawsuit. Academia Sinica held a two-day symposium and produced a policy white paper recommending suspension. In November 2020, the Legislature froze NT$400 million of the project's budget. On January 21, 2021, the Premier officially shelved the entire NT$4.8 billion program.

> 📝 **Curator's Note**
> A democratic government's NT$4.8 billion initiative, halted by its own civil society. This is rare globally. Most countries' digital ID programs follow a "government decides, citizens comply" pattern — India's Aadhaar enrolled 1.2 billion people despite ongoing controversy. Taiwan's New eID remains suspended to this day.

---

## Experts Consulted After the Cards Were Already Printed

The failure wasn't technical — it was procedural.

The project was split into four procurement packages: a planning contract (won by Kuo-Chu Consulting), a card manufacturing contract worth NT$3.29 billion (won by TECO Electric), a systems contract (won by Chunghwa Telecom after multiple failed tenders), and a verification contract (won by DTI). The problem: the latter three contracts were awarded in early 2020 before details had been made public.

A member of the Ministry of the Interior's advisory working group told The Reporter: "The card tender had already been awarded, and only then did they ask us for opinions on the specifications. The Central Engraving and Printing Plant had already finished, and only then did we review the overall plan." He added: "Many people felt the working group was just a rubber stamp." (The Reporter, 2021)

Some members publicly opposed the project. Others simply stopped attending meetings.

This wasn't Taiwan's first failed attempt. In 1998, the KMT government proposed the "National Card" — combining the ID card, health insurance card, and fingerprint data into a single chip card. It was abandoned amid protests from academics and civil society. Two decades later, the faces changed; the core tension didn't.

---

## "A Digital ID Can Be the Foundation for Smart Government — or for an Authoritarian Regime"

Chiou Wen-tsong, a research fellow at Academia Sinica's Institutum Iurisprudentiae, is one of Taiwan's most prominent voices on digital identity. In an interview published by his institute, he explained the fundamental issue:

"The biggest reason is digital footprints. With a paper document, once someone looks at it, no data is left behind unless they copy it down. But in a digital environment, there's always a usage log. As digital footprints accumulate from everyday ID use, the worst case is a surveillance mechanism like China's social credit system." (Institutum Iurisprudentiae interview, 2020)

Lawyer Lin Yu-teng, who assisted the Judicial Reform Foundation's lawsuit, put it more bluntly: "A digital ID can be the foundation for smart government, or it can be infrastructure for an authoritarian regime. The difference between the two is accountability." (The Reporter, 2021)

> 📝 **Curator's Note**
> Taiwan transitioned from authoritarian rule to democracy only about 40 years ago. Sensitivity to state surveillance runs deeper here than in most countries — and not without reason. In early 2021, over 20 million household registration records were found for sale on the dark web. The Ministry of the Interior denied the data came from the government.

---

## Estonia Got It Right — But Taiwan Copied the Wrong Part

When promoting the New eID, the Ministry of the Interior's favorite success story was [[Estonia]]. The Baltic nation of 1.32 million people built what the World Bank calls the world's most successful digital ID system. 99% of government services are available online. They even created an offshore backup of government data — if the country is invaded, the government can continue operating from the cloud.

> **🔢 By the Numbers**
> - Estonia: digital ID adoption rate **97%**, highest digital civic participation rate globally (UN ranking)
> - Taiwan's Citizen Digital Certificate (launched 2003): low adoption rate, primarily used for annual tax filing

But as Chiou pointed out, Taiwan copied Estonia's technology without copying its institutional foundations. Estonia has strict legislation governing ID usage, and — crucially — every citizen can check who accessed their personal data, when, and why. When Estonia suffered a major government data breach in 2007, the government immediately acknowledged the failure and reissued all national ID numbers.

In 2019, after visiting Estonia, the Ministry of the Interior wrote in its trip report: "When errors occur, the government should have the courage to admit mistakes." Then it returned to Taiwan and did nearly the opposite.

---

## The Mask Map: A Different Path to Digital Government

The digital ID stalled, but Taiwan's digital governance landscape was far from barren. Its most distinctive achievement didn't come from the top down — it came from the bottom up, through the [[civic tech and open-source community|g0v (gov-zero)]] movement.

In February 2020, as COVID-19 hit and Taiwan implemented mask rationing, citizens had no way to know which pharmacies still had stock. Wu Chan-wei, founder of the Tainan-based Goodideas Studio, built the first prototype of a convenience store mask map. [[Audrey Tang]], then a minister without portfolio, saw it and immediately connected with Wu. Within days, the government released a real-time open API for pharmacy mask inventory.

> "Audrey Tang has decision-making authority and can write code herself, so we didn't need to go to Taipei to brief some official. Developers could just focus on developing." — Wu Chan-wei (TechNews, 2020)

Over a thousand civic hackers joined an online hackathon. According to NPR, within six days of Wu's first map, every person in Taiwan could check real-time mask stock at any pharmacy. Developers in South Korea and Japan tried to replicate the model — most concluded that the Taiwan experience was nearly impossible to reproduce elsewhere.

The lesson goes beyond technology. The contrast between the digital ID and the mask map reveals a core paradox: **a government's most successful digital services are often the ones it didn't design itself.**

---

## From Minister Without Portfolio to the Ministry of Digital Affairs

Tang's role in the mask map story — as a bridge-builder rather than a commander — carried into her next position. On August 27, 2022, Taiwan's Ministry of Digital Affairs (moda) was officially inaugurated, with Tang as its first minister. She noted that "moda" sounds like the Mandarin word for "motor," expressing her hope that the ministry would become the engine of Taiwan's digital development.

The ministry's creation was partly a consequence of the New eID failure. When the digital ID was shelved, Interior Minister Hsu Kuo-yung admitted he had "no answer" for who the responsible agency should be or whether a new dedicated body was needed. Two years later, moda became that answer.

The structural challenges remain enormous. A white-hat hacker identified as "Howard" told The Reporter about a fundamental problem: "Government officials don't understand technology, so cybersecurity management can't be implemented effectively. Whether in procurement specs or acceptance testing, they simply can't verify whether security problems exist." A 2019 Cabinet audit of 10 government agencies found an average cybersecurity score of just 69.3 out of 100, with 6 out of 10 agencies failing technical assessments. The dedicated cybersecurity workforce across government was running at a 60% vacancy rate — over 1,000 unfilled positions. (The Reporter, 2021)

> **🔢 By the Numbers**
> - Taiwan's Investigation Bureau once posted a senior cybersecurity analyst position requiring a PhD and 9 specialized skills. Monthly salary: **NT$58,000** (roughly US$1,900)
> - In 2019, **590,000** civil servant records from the Ministry of Examination were found exposed online
> - In 2021, over **20 million** Taiwanese household registration records appeared on the dark web

## vTaiwan and the Open Government Experiment

While the digital ID path was blocked, Taiwan advanced further than most countries along a different digital frontier: digital democracy.

Launched in 2015, the vTaiwan platform uses Pol.is — an AI-powered opinion-mapping tool — to enable citizen participation in policymaking. Its most famous case was a 2015 deliberation on Uber regulation — not a negotiation between the government and Uber, but a direct debate among Uber drivers, taxi drivers, and passengers on the platform. The resulting consensus informed legislation. According to CrowdLaw, over 80% of vTaiwan deliberations have led to concrete government action.

Tang was characteristically measured about vTaiwan's role: "This is not a solution for democracy — it's a process for building rough consensus. The rough consensus from vTaiwan can only serve as a reference for policymaking. To create real change, you still need the power of civic participation." (The Reporter, open government feature)

This mirrors the digital ID lesson in reverse — technology alone isn't the answer. Technology without trust is dangerous; trust with rough technology can go surprisingly far.

---

## The NT$4.8 Billion Lesson

After the digital ID was shelved, vendors initially sought over NT$1 billion in compensation. In January 2024, mediation through the Public Construction Commission settled at roughly NT$280 million. Interior Minister Lin Yu-chang acknowledged that "NT$200-something million was the best outcome we could get."

NT$280 million, on top of equipment and facility costs already spent, for a chip card that was never issued. But perhaps what it actually purchased was a lesson for Taiwanese society about digital governance:

**You can build the world's most secure chip, but if citizens don't trust you, the card is just plastic.**

Estonia built 97% digital participation over 30 years of radical transparency. Taiwan's civil society proved it can build a mask map in three days — and stop a NT$4.8 billion government project in its tracks. Both are muscles of digital democracy.

Chiou Wen-tsong, near the end of his Academia Sinica interview, offered what may be the most precise footnote to this entire saga: "You can't just emphasize how wonderful digitization is in other countries and say we should hurry up and copy them, while ignoring the legal institutional foundations those countries painstakingly built." (Institutum Iurisprudentiae interview, 2020)

As of 2026, Taiwan's digital ID still has no timeline. But every tax season, millions of Taiwanese still plug in that Citizen Digital Certificate from 2003, connect a card reader, and complete their most frequent digital interaction with the government. That old card was never hit by a trust crisis — because it never promised too much.
