# Hot Spring Culture: From War Wounds to Healing Empire

In March 1896, when Japanese wanderer Hirata Gengo built "Tenguan Inn" by a creek in Beitou, this former police officer with bullet scars in his knees never imagined that this crude hotel—constructed to treat his foot injuries and beriberi—would launch Taiwan's hot spring civilization. Even less did he expect that this industry, accidentally born from war trauma, would eventually elevate Taiwan to become a healing empire boasting both one of the world's three mud hot springs and one of the world's three undersea hot springs.

> **30-Second Overview:** Taiwan's hot spring culture originated from Japanese colonial military medical needs, evolving from an accidental personal cure into a world-class distribution of 128 hot spring areas. Guanziling's mud springs and Green Island's underwater springs are both rare geological miracles found in only three places worldwide, while Taiwan-style hot springs fuse Japanese healing traditions with local social culture, creating a unique "hot spring+" complex experience.

**Taiwan's hot springs are not a product of the tourism industry, but a civilizational miracle born from the unexpected intersection of war and healing.**

Today's Taiwan features 128 hot spring areas distributed across the island with an astounding density of one per 281 square kilometers—far exceeding that of hot spring powerhouse Japan (approximately 3 per 1000 square kilometers). From Beitou's sulfur acid springs to Guanziling's black mud baths, from Jiaoxi's beauty springs to Green Island's underwater hot springs, Taiwanese have created a unique "Taiwan-style hot spring civilization"—combining Japanese ritualistic essence, Taiwanese warmth, and modern urban convenience.

## A Healing Empire Born from War

The true origin of Taiwan's hot spring development begins with the Japanese military's medical crisis of 1895.

In early Japanese rule over Taiwan, tropical diseases and medical shortages caused devastating losses to Japanese forces. The Governor-General urgently needed to establish a therapeutic system for troops, beginning systematic surveys of hot spring resources across the island. Every discovery of a hot spring triggered immediate spring quality analysis and assessment of feasibility for establishing military recuperation facilities.

**Hirata Gengo was an accidental pioneer in this context.** This former police officer who had participated in the movement to overthrow the shogunate came to Taiwan hoping to strike it rich mining gold. After his application was rejected and he was injured during exploration while simultaneously suffering from beriberi, he couldn't even find a doctor in Keelung. Hearing that there were hot springs in the foothills of Datun Mountain, he headed to Beitou as a last resort.

On November 25, 1895, Hirata Gengo stayed with a Taiwanese family named Chen and began soaking in the creek to heal his wounds. After recovering a month later, he decided to establish a simple hot spring inn on the site—Tenguan Inn—becoming Taiwan's first hot spring operator.

But what truly launched Beitou hot springs was systematic investment by the military administration. In August 1896, Matsumoto Kametaro, finance section chief of Taipei Military Administration, built "Shotoen," specifically for military and political officials' recuperation. Subsequently, Hoyoen and Beitou-kan were established one after another, transforming Beitou from an accidental personal medical discovery into an officially certified therapeutic base.

**This military-medical oriented development model accidentally established the quality DNA of Taiwan's hot spring industry.** To meet military recuperation needs, the Japanese government maintained strict standards for water quality, facilities, and service—standards that continue today and have become competitive advantages for Taiwan's hot spring industry.

> **💡 Did You Know** Without war, Taiwan might not have today's hot spring culture. The historical entanglement of healing and killing created the purest spaces for physical and mental restoration.

## Earth's Exclusive Gift to Taiwan

Taiwan's world-class hot spring status comes from extremely fortunate geological conditions.

Located at the intersection of the Eurasian Plate and Philippine Sea Plate, Taiwan possesses the world's highest density of hot spring distribution. 128 hot spring areas are distributed across 36,000 square kilometers, with an average density of one per 281 square kilometers—far exceeding hot spring powers Japan (approximately 3 per 1000 square kilometers) and Iceland (approximately 1 per 1000 square kilometers).

Even more remarkable is the rarity of spring qualities. Taiwan can experience most of Earth's hot spring types within a 400-kilometer north-south range: from Beitou's acidic sulfate springs (pH 1.5-3) to Jiaoxi's alkaline sodium bicarbonate springs (pH 8-9), with temperatures ranging from 40°C to 75°C and mineral compositions covering sulfur, sodium bicarbonate, chloride, and sulfate types.

**Guanziling's mud hot springs are Taiwan's world-class signature.** Only three places worldwide possess natural mud hot springs: Sicily, Italy; Kagoshima, Japan; and Guanziling, Taiwan. Guanziling's gray-black spring water contains abundant minerals and fine mud particles, with temperatures around 75°C, earning the title "Number One Sacred Spring Under Heaven" during Japanese rule. This mud forms through long-term interaction between gray limestone in geological layers and hot spring water, possessing unique therapeutic effects.

**Green Island's Zhaori Hot Springs represent another Earth miracle.** Only three places worldwide possess underwater hot springs: northern Italy, Yakushima in Kyushu Japan, and Green Island Taiwan. These springs emerge directly from tidal zones, allowing visitors to soak while listening to ocean waves and experiencing unique healing moments at sunrise.

> **📊 Data Source** According to 2024 statistics from the Geological Survey and Mining Management Agency, Ministry of Economic Affairs, among Taiwan's 135 natural hot springs, 52% are sodium bicarbonate springs, 31% are sulfur springs, and 17% are other special spring types. This diversity within the same land area is rare worldwide.

## From Zen to Entertainment: Cultural Evolution of Taiwan-Style Hot Springs

The most fascinating aspect of Taiwan's hot spring culture lies in the "Taiwan-style evolution of Japanese genes."

Traditional Japanese hot springs emphasized the "toji" concept—long-term soaking for medical effects, pursuing pure physical and mental healing. But Taiwanese transformed hot springs into social platforms and family entertainment centers.

Taiwan-style hot spring resorts are typically large-scale, featuring not only basic hot spring pools but also SPAs, fine dining restaurants, KTV, and game rooms. This "hot spring resort" model transformed soaking from personal healing into multi-person social activities. You can book a private bath house for family time, chat and laugh with friends in public pools, or even sing KTV in cabins beside hot springs.

**Private bath house culture is Taiwan's original creation.** Compared to Japanese hot springs' public pool traditions, Taiwan developed refined private spaces, from simple indoor bath houses to luxurious outdoor hot spring baths, priced from 800 to 3000 TWD. This design satisfied Taiwanese preferences for privacy while creating new options for couple dates and family gatherings.

Beitou's Tangse Hot Spring Resort perfectly exemplifies Taiwan-style hot spring characteristics. Here, hot spring pools are categorized by function: 38°C beauty pools, 42°C stress-relief massage pools, and alternating hot-cold "ice and fire" experiences. Visitors can freely choose based on needs—this customized experience represents the essence of Taiwan-style hot springs.

Yet Taiwan-style hot springs retain the core value of healing. Many hot spring areas offer professional SPA treatments, combining aromatherapy, massage, and beauty care to create "hot spring+" complex experiences.

> **📝 Curator's Voice** Taiwan's hot springs reflect island society characteristics—valuing relational connections over individual practice, preferring liveliness over solitude, practicality over formality.

## The Jiaoxi Phenomenon: Urban Miracle of Plains Hot Springs

Jiaoxi represents another innovation in Taiwan's hot spring culture: the "hot spring city."

Unlike most hot springs located in mountainous areas, Jiaoxi sits on the Lanyang Plain, with hot springs emerging throughout the entire urban area. This rare geological condition enabled Jiaoxi to develop the concept of "urban hot springs"—enjoying quality hot springs without leaving convenient living amenities.

**Jiaoxi's sodium bicarbonate springs are acclaimed as "beauty hot springs."** The colorless, odorless spring quality (pH 7-8) is rich in sodium, potassium, and calcium ions, gentle and non-irritating to skin. After soaking, skin becomes smooth and tender, particularly beloved by female visitors. According to 2024 surveys by Internet Temperature, Jiaoxi won the "Most Popular Hot Spring Area in Taiwan" title for three consecutive years, surpassing the historically significant Beitou.

Tangweigou Hot Spring Park demonstrates Jiaoxi's democratic spirit. This free public hot spring park uses bamboo screens for privacy and LED lighting for romantic ambiance, allowing everyone barrier-free access to hot spring culture. Weekend Tangweigou bustles with activity—elderly people chat while soaking feet, couples enjoy fish therapy in spring fish pools, and families cook hot spring eggs together in cooking areas.

After the Hsuehshan Tunnel opened, the journey from Taipei to Jiaoxi takes only one hour, creating a new "commuter hot spring" model. Weekend Jiaoxi streets are filled with Taipei license plates, hot spring hotels are fully booked, and many Taipei residents treat Jiaoxi as their "hot spring living room."

**Jiaoxi's success lies in democratization.** Various price options are available: 100 TWD public foot baths, 150 TWD spring fish therapy, 600 TWD public bath houses, 2000 TWD private hot spring baths, and 8000 TWD premium hot spring suites. Regardless of budget, suitable hot spring experiences can be found.

According to Yilan County Government statistics, Jiaoxi hot spring area received 3.5 million visitors in 2024, with hot spring-related industries generating over 4.5 billion TWD in annual output value—Taiwan's highest economic benefit for a single hot spring area.

## Hot Spring Cuisine: Creative Experiments in Geothermal Cooking

Taiwan's hot spring culture has also developed unique "geothermal cooking" innovations.

**Hot spring eggs represent the classic of geothermal cooking.** Using 60-70°C hot spring water for extended heating, egg whites solidify while yolks remain semi-liquid. This unique cooking method creates special textures that, paired with Japanese soy sauce and seven-spice powder, become essential hot spring area comfort food. At geothermal cooking areas in Beitou Hell Valley and Jiaoxi Tangweigou, visitors can personally cook hot spring eggs, corn, and sweet potatoes, experiencing the joy of "Earth's kitchen."

**Guanziling barrel chicken is a hot spring cuisine classic.** Local chickens slow-roasted using natural geothermal heat develop crispy skin and tender meat, with unique cooking methods imparting subtle mineral aromas to the meat. Paired with Guanziling's specialty mountain pepper and破布子 (pokeweed), this creates irreproducible local flavors.

Innovative operators even attempt "hot spring ramen"—using hot spring water to prepare broths, claiming special sweet flavors. While scientifically difficult to verify hot spring water's actual taste impact, this conceptual combination creates buzz and becomes hot spring area features.

**Hot spring agriculture represents interesting experiments.** Guanziling uses geothermal heat to grow tomatoes, while Beitou uses hot spring water to cultivate water spinach. Though results vary, these attempts demonstrate Taiwan hot spring industry innovation spirit while finding new agricultural possibilities.

> **🍽️ Food Culture Perspective** Hot spring cuisine isn't just about satisfying appetites—it's concrete practice of human-land connections. Using Earth's thermal energy to cook food represents both the most ancient and most modern cooking methods.

## Smart Hot Springs in the Digital Era

Technology is redefining Taiwan's hot spring experiences.

**Smart water quality monitoring systems** make soaking safer. High-end hot spring resorts in Taipei's Beitou install real-time monitoring equipment, allowing visitors to view current spring water pH values, mineral content, and temperatures on screens. This transparency enhances consumer confidence while driving industry-wide quality improvements.

**Online reservation systems** solve hot spring peak season supply-demand imbalances. Apps enable advance bath house bookings, preferred time slots and room types, avoiding on-site waiting. This contactless service proved especially important during the pandemic, helping many operators survive difficult periods.

**Themed hot springs** attract younger generations. Creative combinations like petal springs, milk springs, wine springs, and coffee springs may deviate from tradition but create buzz in social media times. These Instagram-friendly designs attract young visitors, injecting new vitality into traditional hot spring industries.

**VR hot spring experiences** represent the next frontier. Some operators experiment with providing virtual reality experiences while soaking, allowing visitors to tour worldwide scenery or experience meditation guidance courses while bathing.

Smart hot springs aren't meant to replace traditional experiences but to lower barriers, enhance quality, and expand customer bases. The core remains physical and mental healing—technology merely provides better vehicles.

## Sustainability Challenges: Protecting Earth's Gifts

Taiwan's hot spring industry faces critical sustainable development challenges.

**Over-development poses the greatest concern.** Extensive construction in popular hot spring areas may affect natural spring output and water quality. The complete construction ban in Nantou's Lushan Hot Springs due to geological disasters serves as a serious warning against over-development. Environmental groups estimate approximately 30% of Taiwan's hot spring areas face risks of excessive development.

**Climate change** brings new threats. Extreme rainfall may affect hot spring water quality, while prolonged droughts could reduce water output. Some hot spring areas already show decreased temperatures and reduced output, requiring more precise monitoring and protection measures.

Current "Hot Spring Law" establishes basic development controls, but faces emerging business models (urban hot springs, hot spring guesthouses, smart hot springs) requiring more detailed regulations. Particularly in indigenous areas, hot spring development needs to balance economic development with cultural protection.

Japan, South Korea, and Iceland actively promote hot spring tourism. Taiwan must enhance international competitiveness while maintaining quality. Developing eco-hot springs, cultural hot springs, and health hot springs as differentiation strategies may provide breakthrough paths.

> **⚠️ Sustainability Thinking** Hot springs are Earth's gift to Taiwan. We must learn to cherish and use rather than squander and consume. Future generations also have the right to enjoy this warmth from Earth's core.

## Hot Spring Etiquette: Balancing Modern and Traditional

When experiencing Taiwan's hot spring culture, basic etiquette remains important.

Shower before entering pools—this shows basic respect for water quality maintenance and other visitors. Use soap to thoroughly clean your body, ensuring personal hygiene. Control soaking time, with recommended 10-15 minute sessions followed by rest, avoiding excessive soaking causing physical discomfort. If experiencing dizziness, heart palpitations, or discomfort, immediately leave hot spring pools for shaded rest areas.

Maintain quiet atmospheres, avoiding loud conversations affecting others. Hot springs are healing spaces where quiet environments facilitate physical and mental relaxation. Keep phones on silent mode, avoiding poolside phone calls.

Respect facility regulations, including whether swimwear is required and whether towels may be brought. Private bath houses have time limits—please return punctually, allowing next guests smooth usage.

Taiwan's hot spring culture evolved from accidental military medical needs a century ago into today's multifaceted life experiences combining healing, leisure, and socializing. In this process, we not only preserved Japanese hot spring refinement but also integrated Taiwanese creativity and human warmth, creating the world's unique "Taiwan-style hot spring civilization."

When you next soak in hot springs, feel not only geothermal warmth but also healing melodies composed over centuries through cultural fusion, geological gifts, and humanistic innovation—this represents Taiwan's hot spring culture's true value.
