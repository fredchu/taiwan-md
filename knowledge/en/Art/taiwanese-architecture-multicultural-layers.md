# Taiwanese Architecture: Multicultural Layers

The history of Taiwanese architecture is like a three-dimensional cultural encyclopedia, with each era leaving different architectural imprints on this land. From indigenous stone slab houses to Dutch and Spanish forts, from Japanese colonial government buildings to postwar modernist trends, and contemporary architects' innovative practices, Taiwanese architecture presents a unique landscape of multicultural integration. These buildings are not merely shelters from wind and rain but cultural identity carriers, witnesses to historical memory, and concrete manifestations of the relationship between people and land.

## Wisdom of the Earth: Indigenous Architecture

### Architectural Philosophy of Coexistence with Nature

At the source of Taiwan's architectural history, indigenous architecture demonstrates the purest environmental wisdom. These buildings do not pursue eternal monumentality but deeply integrate with the natural environment, embodying the concept of sustainable development. Indigenous architectural wisdom stems from deep understanding of the land, keen perception of climate, and a life philosophy of harmonious coexistence with nature.

Different ethnic groups developed distinctive architectural forms in response to different geographical environments. Mountain tribes utilized abundant stone and timber resources, while coastal groups made good use of bamboo and thatch. Each architectural type represents a precise response to the environment, showcasing humanity's infinite wisdom in adapting to nature.

### Stone Slab Houses: Memory of the Mountains

The stone slab houses of the Paiwan and Rukai tribes are among the most representative forms of Taiwan's indigenous architecture. These buildings use local materials, utilizing abundant slate resources from mountainous areas, constructed through dry stone stacking methods without any binding materials, yet able to stand firm for centuries.

The architectural wisdom of stone slab houses manifests in multiple layers: first, the choice of materials—slate provides excellent thermal insulation properties, warm in winter and cool in summer; second, structural ingenuity—the overlapping stacking of stone slabs forms a stable load-bearing system capable of resisting strong mountain winds and earthquakes; third, spatial configuration—the transitional treatment between indoor and outdoor spaces protects privacy while maintaining connection with nature.

Stone slab houses are not only living spaces but also places of cultural transmission. The indoor hearth serves as the center for family gatherings, while wall carvings and decorations carry the history and beliefs of the tribe. Each stone slab records the dialogue between the people and the land; each space tells of ancestral wisdom.

### Stilt Houses: Architecture of Wind

Indigenous peoples of plains and coastal areas, such as the Amis and Puyuma tribes, developed stilt house architectural forms. These buildings raise the main living spaces above ground, preventing moisture and insects while responding to threats from floods and tides.

The design of stilt houses demonstrates precise mastery of climate: elevated spaces promote air circulation, effectively lowering indoor temperatures; the transparent ground level can serve as workspace or livestock shelter, fully utilizing vertical space; sloped roofs with protruding eaves effectively drain water and provide shade.

The flexibility of this architectural form is also noteworthy. Timber and bamboo structures allow for dismantling and reassembly; when groups need to migrate, buildings can move with them, embodying nomadic culture characteristics. This "light architecture" concept coincides with contemporary sustainable architecture principles, demonstrating the forward-thinking nature of indigenous culture.

### Cultural Significance of Space

The value of indigenous architecture lies not only in its technical aspects but also in the cultural content it carries. Building orientations often correspond to cosmology, spatial divisions reflect social structures, and decorative patterns tell mythological legends. These buildings are living cultural carriers, transforming abstract cultural concepts into concrete spatial experiences.

Unfortunately, with the advancement of modernization, traditional indigenous architecture has gradually disappeared. In recent years, under the wave of cultural revival, many tribes have begun rebuilding traditional architecture, not only to preserve cultural memory but also hoping to reconnect people with the land through architecture and recover lost life wisdom.

## Fortresses from the Sea: Dutch and Spanish Remains

### Architectural Traces of the Colonial Era

In the 17th century, Spanish and Dutch colonizers successively arrived in Taiwan. To consolidate colonial rule, they built fortresses at important ports and strategic locations. These European-style military buildings marked Taiwan's first contact with Western architectural technology and represent an important turning point in Taiwan's architectural history.

The construction technology of these fortresses was completely different from traditional indigenous architecture. Thick stone walls, precise geometric forms, and standardized architectural components all displayed the characteristics of European military architecture. These buildings not only changed Taiwan's urban skyline but also introduced new architectural concepts and technologies.

### Anping Old Fort: Layers of Time

Anping Old Fort (originally Fort Zeelandia) was the most important fortress built by the Dutch in Taiwan and marks the beginning of Western-style architecture in Taiwan. This fortress experienced different regimes—Dutch, Koxinga, Qing Dynasty, and Japanese colonial periods—with each era leaving traces, forming complex historical layers.

Fort Zeelandia during the Dutch period adopted a star fort design, the most advanced military architectural form in 16th-17th century Europe. Walls several meters thick could resist cannon attacks; protruding bastion designs eliminated defensive dead angles; multi-story internal buildings provided ample storage and living space.

During the Koxinga period, the Zheng regime renovated the fortress, incorporating Chinese architectural elements. After Qing rule, due to shifts in military focus, the fort gradually fell into disrepair. During the Japanese colonial period, the Japanese built new structures near the original site, further altering the fort's original appearance.

Today's Anping Old Fort is a composite of architectural elements from multiple eras. Although the original Dutch castle no longer exists, remaining wall foundations and warehouse ruins still allow us to feel the architectural atmosphere of that great maritime age.

### Fort San Domingo: Cultural Convergence

Fort San Domingo in Tamsui is another important colonial-period building. Originally built by the Spanish, later reconstructed by the Dutch, renovated during the Qing period, and expanded with new buildings during the British consulate period. This architectural complex witnessed nearly 400 years of Taiwan's historical changes and serves as the best example of multicultural integration.

The architectural character of Fort San Domingo lies in its multiple personalities: the main fort maintains the heavy style of the Dutch period, the later British consulate demonstrates Victorian architectural aesthetics, while the Chinese-style gate added during the Qing period brings Eastern charm to the entire complex.

This multicultural architectural form epitomizes Taiwan's architectural history. On this land, different cultural architectural traditions met, collided, and merged, creating unique Taiwanese architectural characteristics.

## Imperial Imprints: The Splendor of Japanese Colonial Architecture

### Architectural Revolution of Modernization

After Japan began ruling Taiwan in 1895, to demonstrate colonial government authority and promote modernization, large-scale urban construction and architectural activities were launched in Taiwan. Buildings from this period not only introduced Western modern architectural concepts technically but also demonstrated unique East-West fusion characteristics aesthetically.

Public buildings during the Japanese colonial period pursued magnificent and spectacular effects, symbolizing regime authority and civilizational progress through architecture. These buildings mostly used combinations of red brick and washed stone with exquisite decorative details, forming the so-called "Tatsuno-style architecture."

### Moriyama Matsunosuke: Shaper of Taiwanese Architecture

Among the many architects during the Japanese colonial period, Moriyama Matsunosuke (1869-1949) was the most influential. This architect, a student of Tatsuno Kingo, designed important buildings in Taiwan including the Presidential Office (former Governor-General's Office), Taipei Prefecture Hall, Taichung Prefecture Hall, and Tainan Prefecture Hall, profoundly influencing Taiwan's urban landscape.

Moriyama's architectural style inherited his master Tatsuno Kingo's "Tatsuno-style architecture" characteristics: heavy red brick bases, exquisite washed stone facades, rich decorative details, and symmetrical facade compositions. However, in his Taiwan works, he also incorporated considerations for tropical climate, such as deeply recessed window designs, high ceilings, and ventilated corridors.

### Presidential Office: Symbol of Power

The current Presidential Office (former Taiwan Governor-General's Office) is Moriyama's masterpiece and one of Taiwan's most symbolic buildings. Completed in 1919, this building demonstrates colonial government authority through its magnificent scale and exquisite details.

The building adopts a "回" (hui) character-shaped plan configuration, with the central high tower symbolizing the center of power, surrounded by low building clusters. Externally, the combination of red brick and washed stone creates rich layers and textures. Facade elements like arched windows, gables, and towers all reflect European classical architectural influence.

This building's design was clearly influenced by Tokyo Station. In fact, both buildings are representative works of Tatsuno-style architecture, closely related in design concepts and decorative techniques. This cross-border transmission of architectural language reflects the characteristics of cultural transplantation during the colonial period.

### Taipei Guest House: Elegant Juxtaposition of Japanese and Western Styles

Taipei Guest House (former Taiwan Governor's Official Residence) is another important Japanese colonial period building. This building demonstrates the characteristic juxtaposition of Japanese and Western architecture—part using Japanese-style wooden structure, another part Western-style brick construction, with both styles harmoniously coexisting within the same building complex.

The building's garden design is also exceptional, combining the refinement of Japanese gardens with the splendor of Western gardens. This "Japanese-Western juxtaposition" design concept reflects Japan's acceptance of Western culture and persistence of indigenous culture after the Meiji Restoration, also influencing Taiwan's architectural development direction.

### Railway Stations: Aesthetics of Transportation Architecture

Railway station buildings during the Japanese colonial period were another important architectural type. These stations were not only transportation facilities but also showcases of modern civilization. Although stations varied in scale, they all embodied unified design concepts: the combination of functionality and aesthetics.

The old Taipei Railway Station (demolished), Hsinchu Railway Station, and Taichung Railway Station were all representative works of this period. These buildings typically adopted symmetrical facade designs with tall central clock towers and spacious entrance porticos. Architectural styles fused European classical and modern elements, appearing both dignified and approachable.

### Railway Department: Elegance of Half-Timber Construction

The Taipei Railway Department (now National Taiwan Museum Railway Department Park) is another important work by Moriyama. This building adopted English country house-style "half-timber construction," with a red brick base below and exposed wooden post construction on white walls above, creating unique visual effects.

This architectural style was quite rare in Taiwan's public buildings, demonstrating the architect's innovative spirit. The building's entrance design is also distinctive, with triangular gables echoing flanking tall towers, and roofs using natural slate tiles. The overall design is elegant and refined.

## Modernism's Transplantation and Localization

### Postwar Reconstruction and Modernization

After Taiwan's retrocession in 1945, facing reconstruction from war damage and rapid urbanization needs, modernist architectural trends were introduced to Taiwan from the 1950s, becoming the mainstream of postwar architectural development. Buildings from this period pursued functionality, economy, and rationalization, reflecting society's strong desire for modernization.

Early postwar architecture was mainly government-led, addressing housing shortages and inadequate public facilities. Although these buildings were aesthetically modest, they were quite practical functionally, laying foundations for Taiwan's modernization construction.

### The Age of Concrete

In the 1960s-70s, with rapid economic growth, Taiwan entered an era of large-scale construction. Modernist architecture reached its peak during this period, with reinforced concrete becoming the mainstream building material. These buildings pursued simple geometric forms, emphasizing functionality and economy, reflecting the rational spirit of that era.

Representative buildings from this period include Zhongshan Hall, Sun Yat-Sen Memorial Hall, and Chiang Kai-Shek Memorial Hall. Although these buildings differed stylistically, they all embodied basic modernist principles: function determines form, simplified decoration, and application of new materials and technologies.

However, pure International Style modernism faced challenges related to climate, culture, and society during its transplantation to Taiwan. Architects began thinking about how to combine modernist principles with local conditions, developing modern architecture with Taiwanese characteristics.

### Postmodernist Reflection

From the 1980s, postmodernist trends influenced Taiwan's architectural community. Architects began reflecting on pure functionalism's limitations, renewed attention to architecture's cultural content and symbolic meaning. Buildings from this period were more diverse formally and culturally emphasized locality.

This transformation reflected changes in Taiwanese society: with economic development maturity and democratization progress, Taiwanese people began reexamining their cultural identity, and architecture became an important medium for expressing cultural identity.

## Innovation and Reflection in Contemporary Architecture

### Huang Sheng-yuan and Fieldoffice: Poetic Architecture

Entering the 21st century, Taiwan's architectural community produced a new generation of representative architects, among whom Huang Sheng-yuan has undoubtedly received the most international attention. His Fieldoffice Architects, with its unique architectural philosophy and practice, has earned international reputation for Taiwanese architecture.

Huang's architectural philosophy emphasizes dialogue between architecture and environment, values user experience, and pursues poetic spatial quality. Most of his works are located in Yilan, where through long-term local cultivation, he developed unique architectural vocabulary. He says: "Once a person stands on the roof, they easily gain insight." Fieldoffice architecture makes it easy for people to walk onto roofs, change perspectives, and reunderstand the relationship between environment and self.

### The Yilan Experience: Local Modernity

Fieldoffice's practice in Yilan is called the "Yilan Experience." These works include Yilan Social Welfare Center, Luodong Cultural Workshop, and Jiaoxi Household Registration Office, each building embodying deep understanding and innovative interpretation of local characteristics.

The common characteristics of these buildings are: openness, accessibility, and integration with environment. Architecture is no longer a closed container but an open platform; no longer a symbol of authority but democratic space; no longer opposing environment but dialoguing with nature. This architectural philosophy reflects Taiwan society's democratization process and awakening environmental consciousness.

Huang's design for Sakura Cemetery even moved Finnish architectural master Juhani Pallasmaa to tears. In this space at the boundary between life and death, architecture, with its quiet power, makes people rethink the meaning of life.

### Kris Yao: Persistence in Refined Modernism

Kris Yao is another important contemporary Taiwanese architect, known for his refined modernist style. Since the 1990s, he has been a main helmsman of Taiwan's architectural aesthetic movement, dedicating himself to redirecting late-period commercially-oriented postmodernism back toward pure modernism.

Yao's works include Lanyang Museum, Kaohsiung World Games Stadium, and National Palace Museum Southern Branch. These works all embody his persistent pursuit of architectural quality: precise proportions, beautiful lines, pure materials, and exquisite details. His architectural language is both modern and elegant, both international and with Eastern charm.

Lanyang Museum is one of Yao's representative works. The building's form derives from Yilan's coastal reefs, creating a modern yet locally characteristic architectural image through abstract treatment. The building's skin uses different colored stones, echoing Yilan's changing skies and demonstrating the architect's keen observation of local culture.

### Sustainable Architecture Exploration

Facing global climate change challenges, Taiwan's architectural community has also begun actively exploring sustainable architecture possibilities. Green building label promotion, solar building experiments, and rediscovery of traditional architectural wisdom are all part of this effort.

This shift is not only technical innovation but also value transformation. Architects have begun reexamining the relationship between architecture and environment, thinking about how to reduce building environmental impact through design and create healthier, more sustainable living environments.

## Temples of Faith: The Art of Religious Architecture

### Cultural Depth of Religious Architecture

In Taiwan's architectural spectrum, temple architecture holds a special position. These religious buildings are not only places of faith but also gathering places for traditional crafts, preservers of cultural memory, and centers of community life. Taiwan's temple architecture integrates different architectural traditions from Fujian, Chaozhou, Hakka, and others while being influenced by indigenous culture, forming unique Taiwanese temple style.

The value of temple architecture lies not only in its religious function but also in the artistic value and cultural content it carries. Traditional crafts such as stone carving, wood carving, painting, cut-and-paste work (jianzhan 剪黏), and Cochin ceramics (jiaozhi tao 交趾陶) receive their most complete expression in temple architecture, forming Taiwan's richest repository of folk art.

### Artistic Halls of Carved Beams and Painted Pillars

The most prominent feature of Taiwanese temple architecture is its rich and complex decorative art. "Carved beams and painted pillars, golden and brilliant" is not only aesthetic pursuit but also expression of reverence for deities. Every corner of a temple may hide exquisite artworks; every detail carries profound cultural meaning.

Stone carving art holds an important position in temple architecture. Dragon pillars, lion bases, and flower-bird reliefs demonstrate stone carvers' exquisite skills. These stone carving works not only pursue technical perfection but are also rich in symbolic meaning: dragons symbolize imperial power and divine authority, lions represent protection and evil dispelling, and flowers and birds signify auspiciousness and beauty.

Wood carving art is even more delicate and refined. Dougong (斗拱 bracket sets), sparrow braces (què tì 雀替), and shrines are all venues for wood carving art display. Traditional wood carving themes include mythological legends, historical stories, flora and fauna, each theme having specific cultural content and educational significance.

### Painting: Epics on Walls

Temple painting is an important component of Taiwan traditional art. Painting contains two meanings: "cai 彩" refers to lacquer work on wooden structures, while "hui 繪" refers to pure fine art creation. In temple architecture, painting not only has the practical function of protecting wood but is also important decorative art.

Traditional painting themes are diverse, including mythological stories, historical allusions, flowers, birds, insects, fish, and landscape scenes. These painted works are like folk comic strips, presenting complex cultural content visually, fulfilling functions of education and cultural transmission.

Door god paintings are an important category of temple painting. Door gods not only have protective religious significance but also demonstrate painters' highest artistic skills. Different door gods have different formal characteristics and meanings, embodying rich folk belief culture.

### Cut-and-Paste and Cochin Ceramics: Three-Dimensional Poems

Cut-and-paste work and Cochin ceramics are decorative crafts unique to Taiwanese temple architecture. Cut-and-paste involves cutting various colored porcelain pieces and assembling them into patterns, while Cochin ceramics use low-temperature fired colored ceramic sculptures. These two crafts are often used together, creating rich and colorful three-dimensional decorations on temple roofs and walls.

The themes of these three-dimensional decorations often draw from folk stories and opera, such as "Romance of the Three Kingdoms," "Journey to the West," and "Investiture of the Gods." Craftsmen transform these story plots into three-dimensional artworks with not only exquisite skills but also extremely rich imagination.

### Sacred Spatiality

The spatial configuration of temple architecture embodies profound religious and cultural content. From the front hall (san chuan dian 三川殿) to the main hall, from the plaza to the rear hall, each space has specific functions and symbolic meanings. This spatial sequence design creates a gradual experience from secular to sacred, guiding believers through spiritual purification processes.

The decorative density of temple architecture also embodies the specialness of religious space. Unlike modern architecture's pursuit of simplicity, temple architecture pursues richness and magnificence, creating sacred atmosphere through visual impact. This "maximalist" aesthetic is precisely the unique charm of Taiwanese temple architecture.

## Taiwanese Spirit in Architecture

The development trajectory of Taiwanese architecture reflects Taiwan culture's diversity and inclusiveness. From indigenous ecological wisdom to colonial-period cultural transplantation, from rational pursuit in modernization processes to contemporary architectural innovation practice, each stage has added new layers and meanings to Taiwanese architecture.

These buildings are not only creations of physical space but also carriers of cultural memory. They witness Taiwan society's changes, record different eras' values and aesthetic concepts, and demonstrate Taiwanese people's creativity and resilience when facing challenges.

In today's globalization, Taiwanese architecture faces new challenges and opportunities. How to maintain local characteristics while internationalizing, how to inherit traditional culture during modernization processes, how to find balance between economic development and environmental protection—these are all questions contemporary Taiwanese architects need to consider.

Fortunately, from Huang Sheng-yuan's poetic architecture to Kris Yao's refined modernism, from traditional temple preservation and restoration to green building innovation practice, Taiwanese architecture is responding to these challenges in its unique way. These efforts not only enrich Taiwan's architectural culture but also contribute Taiwanese wisdom and experience to world architectural development.

The story of Taiwanese architecture continues to be written. On this island where multiple cultures converge, architecture will continue playing the role of cultural bridge, connecting past and future, East and West, tradition and innovation. This is the charm of Taiwanese architecture—seeking unity in diversity, maintaining continuity in change, insisting on locality in globalization, creating Taiwan's unique architectural culture.
